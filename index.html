The

Other Side

A Story of Racial Inequality in America

Introduction

"Sitting at the table doesn't make you a diner, unless you eat some of what's on that plate. Being here in America doesn't make you an American...

I'm not an American. I'm one of the twenty-two million black people who are the victims of Americanism."

– Malcolm X

The state of American politics rests amidst a strange equilibrium: cognizant of nation-wide, structural issues, but incapable of enacting targeted reforms. The past years' discussions surrounding criminal justice were no exception. While most can identify what terms like systemic racism entail in the abstract, hardly anyone can grasp the scope of how far structural injustice pervades every American institution. This project aims to pull that curtain back: to examine the legislative causes and symptoms of systemic racism in the United States.

This is a look into the other side of America.

Part 1: Wealth
Economic Barriers

The first component of systemic racism is its most visible—the racial wealth gap. While white colonists were able to accumulate wealth throughout the early history of the United States, Black families were prohibited from profiting off of their labor. The result is a centuries-long cap on the economic growth of Black communities.

This ceiling was created by 246 years of chattel slavery, which erased the ability for African Americans to accumulate lifetime wealth. Even after its abolishment, incidents like Tulsa's Greenwood District Massacre and the collapse of the Freedman's Savings Bank undermined economic recovery. Such tragedies were amplified by discriminatory federal legislation—like the G.I. Bill, the New Deal's protection extemptions, and Jim Crow segregation 1 —stifling any opportunities to acquire wealth until the early '90s.

Wealth was taken from these communities before they had a chance to earn it.

Generational Wealth

One of the symptoms of the racial wealth gap is the lack of generational wealth in African-American communities. Black families not only have less wealth compared to white households, but also inherit less. White households are twice as likely to receive an inheritance; and on average, an inheritance increases a white household's net wealth by $104,000, compared to only $4,000 among Black ones. 2

1800s

The disparity is a result of deliberate legislation, not chance. Even after slavery was abolished, racial stigma and segregationist laws locked Black workers out of employment opportunities. The 19th century's physical shackles evolved into the 20th century's Jim Crow laws.

Occupational Discrimination

Less overt forms of economic inequality in employment exist to this day. Black men earn less then equally qualified white men across all occupations; in fact, they're the only racial group that does not achieve occupational parity at any level. (They only earn 87 cents for every dollar a white man earns. ) This institutionalized inequality also contributes to the devaluation of Black households, which are worth 60% less than their white counterparts. 3

Unemployment rates are equally skewed. Prior to the 2020 recession, 5.8% of Black Americans were unemployed, compared to only 3.1% of whites. 4 Disparities in employment outcomes become permanent over time. 20.8% of African Americans fall below the national poverty line annually—that's 12.7% more than their white peers. 5

Part 2: Housing
Redlining

The racial wealth gap is only one part of the story. Post-abolition, government-sponsored housing segregation stunted the economic recovery of Black communities. From the 1930's onwards, banks often dliberately denied mortages to people of color. This practice—dubbed "redlining" for its colored markers—created cities capable of segregation sans overt enforcement. The results were devastating: Black communities lost out on $212,000 per household in personal wealth. 6

The legacy of redlining is painfully visible to this day. It's the reason Black communities are often crowded into under-developed areas of urban landscapes, where job opportunities, educational facilities, and public services barely exist. Meanwhile, majority-white communities thrive in the suburbs, in areas that were inaccessible to Black people until the 21st century.

Segregated Schooling

Redlining might be a relatively known phenomenon, but its impacts on Black communities are far more insidious—and covert. Where you live determines everything from your education to your physical fitness, a fact that Black Americans are reminded of daily. Because of decades-old redlining, over 50% of children are still forced into segregated schools, where over 75% of students are either white or non-white.

In addition, because of the devaluation of Black property via property taxes, non-white schools are terminally behind their counterparts. School districts that serve predominantly students of color receive $23 billion less in funding than mostly white school districts, despite serving the same amount of students. 7

That funding disparity decides the fate of millions of young students each year. Graduation rates for Black students are 5% lower than their white counterparts 8 , and college enrollment rates follow a similar trend.

Food Deserts

The consequences of housing discrimination don't stop at schooling. Redlining is also responsible for the existence of food deserts: areas where healthy meal options are inaccessible for miles. These urban pockets are devoid of fresh produce or nutritional options, instead favoring pre-packaged junk.

The result is a disturbing inequalities in health outcomes: Black Americans are 51% more likely to be obese, as compared to their white counterparts. 9 (Perhaps even more unbelievably, only 8% of African-Americans live in a census tract with a supermarket.) 10

Environmental Inequality

Perhaps the most sinister effect of redlining is its invisible perpetuation of environmental racism. In the 1900's, local governments refused to invest in parks and other natural resources, creating adverse health outcomes in Black neighborhoods. Black children in America are twice as likely to develop asthma as their peers, and African-American neighborhoods are exposed to 50% more pollution than white ones. 11

Minority communities were also deliberately placed next to dangerous pollution centers. Majority-Black housing is often situated much closer to oil wells and production plants—exposing Black people to 1.5 times more particulate matter than whites. 12

Part 3: Criminal Justice
Policing

Wealth, housing, schooling, nutrition—all these institutions are products of systemic racism, not its source. But the origin of institutional inequality in the United States is criminal justice: an institution designed to keep Black Americans enslaved since its inception.

In 1838, America's police force was created to capture fugitive slaves and return them to the horrors of the plantation; and even in the 21st century, its effect remains the same among Black communities. People of color in America are executed by police departments almost every single day. George Floyd, Tamir Rice, Rashad Brooks, Treyvon Martin, Breonna Taylor, Atatiana Jefferson—the names go on and on.

Now
Then

Policing in America is broken. Yet, for many, its dangers are not readily apparent. Here are the numbers:

Black people are 3 times more likely to be killed by police than white people. Yet, they're 1.3 times more likely to be unarmed. 10% of the country's largest police departments kill Black people at a higher rate than the national murder rate. 13 Black males are 5 times as likely to be stopped by the police for no apparent reason as whites. 14 They're also 6 times more likely to be targed by stop-and-frisk policies. 15 About 1 in every 1000 Black men can expect to be killed by the police. 16 In some areas of the country, police kill Black people at a rate six times higher than they target whites. 17

Less than 1% of police officers involved in such cases are ever convicted. 18

Incarceration

Police brutality is just one symptom of a structurally racist criminal justice system. Incarceration rates rates are equally skewed.

Not only are Black Americans more likely to be racially profiled by the police, they're also more likely to be convicted of crimes they didn't commit. African Americans are incarcerated in state prisons across the country at more than 5 times the rate of whites. 19 In 2014, more than 2.3 million Black males were part of the prison population. In fact, 1 in every 3 Black males born today will go to prison at some point in their lives. 20 Thanks to three-strikes legislation, the War on Drugs, and manatory minimums, Black males are also more likely to have a criminal record than everything from a bachelor's degree to military service. 21

But the results of mass incarceration stretch far beyond the sentence itself—and they're often covert as well. Black males with criminal records are often disqualified from employment, health insurance, and gun ownership, effectively relegating them to a second-class citizen. Convicted felons also lose the right to vote in most states, erasing any potential for reform. Almost 7.4% of the adult Black population is disenfranchised, compared to 1.8% of the rest of the country. That's 1 in 13 Black Americans who will never be able to vote again. 22

Sentencing Disparities

As with every other facet of systemic inequality in America, mass incarceration is also a product of racial stigma—in this case, in sentencing disparities. Black men who commit the same crimes as white men recieve federal prison sentences that are nearly 20% longer. 23 In addition, Black offenders are 75% more likely to face a charge carrying a mandatory minimum sentence than a white offender who committed the same crime. 24

This disparity is present at every division of the criminal justice system. 22% of Black defendants who kill white victims are executed; meanwhile, only 8% of non-Black defendants are given the same treatment. 25 Black men are sentenced on drug charges 13.4 times more than white men. 26 Even homicides involving Black victims are 23% less likely to result in a conviction. 27

The Solution

This is the reality that Black Americans face—a reality filled with omnipresent danger and little opportunity for escape. Unfortunately, education's only part of the path to ending systemic racism. Even if we know about the oft-hidden dangers of being Black in America, progress requires our unending vigilance.

However, there are other ways that you can support the cause. Consider donating either your time or resources, or supporting Black-owned businesses. You can also sign petitions to pressure your local government into instituting reform. To further educate yourself, try looking into what allyship means, the work of Black activists, or a reading list for anti-racist action.


This was the story of the other side of America. Only time will tell whether it remains true or not.
